The Spot That Was Never Permanent

The longest-lived storm in the solar system has no fixed matter in it — and the first one we actually called “permanent” is already gone.

You have seen it even if you’ve never looked through a telescope: the pale cinnamon eye on Jupiter’s southern flank, the most famous piece of weather on any world. It is wide enough to swallow the Earth whole. It has been turning for as long as anyone alive can remember, and for many lifetimes before that — a single storm that seems to have simply always been there.

And there is nothing there.

Not nothing as in empty. The Great Red Spot is a deep churn of hydrogen, ammonia, and trace chemicals, with winds along its rim running several hundred miles an hour. Nothing as in: there is no object. No body of matter that has lasted three centuries. The spot is a pattern — a high-pressure vortex, gas wheeling counterclockwise once every six days — and the gas itself does not stay. It flows in, loops around, and leaves. What persists is the wheeling, not the wheel.

Pull back and it gets stranger. For all its width, the spot is shallow: Juno’s gravity measurements put its roots at roughly 300 kilometers, a thin lens laid against the cloud tops of a planet 140,000 kilometers across. It sits pinned between two of Jupiter’s jet streams — one to its north, one to its south, racing in opposite directions — like a ball bearing spun between two belts. And it eats. Smaller storms, each half the size of Earth, drift into it and are pulled apart and absorbed; in recent years it has swallowed as many as two dozen in a single year. The spot runs on the weather around it. It is less something Jupiter has than something Jupiter does.

Here is the humbling part. In the 1660s, astronomers including Giovanni Cassini recorded a dark mark at just this latitude, and for decades they tracked what they called the Permanent Spot. Then, after 1713, the records fall silent. For more than a century — nothing. The Great Red Spot we know doesn’t reappear in the drawings until the 1830s. In 2024, a team led by Agustín Sánchez-Lavega worked back through more than three centuries of observations and reached the uncomfortable conclusion: these are almost certainly not the same storm. Cassini’s Permanent Spot died. Jupiter drew a new one in nearly the same place.

So the most enduring weather in the solar system is less than two centuries old — younger than the United States — and the original, the one actually named “permanent,” is long gone. The permanence was never in the storm. It was in the conditions: a planet whose winds, left to run, tend to tie this particular knot.

And the knot is restless. Across the last century the spot has been shrinking — about 39,000 kilometers wide in 1879, around 14,000 today, and rounder than it was, an oval easing toward a circle. Then, watching it closely from December 2023 to March 2024, Hubble caught it doing something no one had predicted: oscillating. Over roughly 90 days the entire spot swelled, shrank, and swelled again — jiggling, NASA’s team said, “like a stress ball,” squeezed by the jet streams above and below the way filling bulges from an overstuffed sandwich. Why the cycle runs 90 days, nobody yet knows. The largest storm in the solar system has a pulse, and we are only now learning to take it.

This is what earns the Great Red Spot a place here. It is the grandest standing wave you can find with a backyard telescope: a shape held steady by the very stuff streaming through it, and beautiful precisely because it never holds still. We like to think that lasting means staying — that the durable things are the solid ones, the granite and the gold. Jupiter disagrees. The spot has outlived nearly every solid thing built by human hands in the same span, and it has done it by never being solid at all — by being only a way the wind moves, redrawn molecule by molecule, year upon year, on a planet that cannot stop spinning.

It will not last forever. It may already be winding down. But if it goes, the safe bet — Cassini’s spot would tell you — is that Jupiter, in a century or three, ties the knot again.
